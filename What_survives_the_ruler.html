What Survives the Ruler

Origin, unit, and orientation behind the signed lengths a = Py − T

A note for Mats

When we write a = Py − T for the drop from the top edge down to P, three background choices are folded silently into those coordinates: where 0 sits (the origin), how long “one” is (the unit), and which way the axis counts (the orientation). None of the three is visible in the picture, yet each is a free choice. This note isolates them and says exactly what each one does — and does not do — to the signed lengths and to their ratios, then locates the cross-ratio one floor above. Everything below is elementary and machine-checked; the ledger at the end prices each line.

Signed length (difference)
a = Py − T — a number attached to an ordered pair of points on a line, available only once a coordinate has been chosen.
Origin
the point assigned coordinate 0. Free to place anywhere; it is the additive constant k.
Unit
the length assigned coordinate 1. Free to choose; it is the scale factor s.
Orientation
the direction of increasing coordinate — the sign of s. SVG counts y downward.
A coordinate change on a line
is y → s·y + k with s ≠ 0 — the affine group of the line. Translation is the k part; scaling and orientation are the s part.

The figure stacks three things, each blind to more of that freedom than the one below it. Here are the rungs.

1Differences forget the origin

Shift every coordinate by a constant and the signed length does not feel it.

why Under y → y + k:  (Py + k) − (T + k) = Py − T. The origin cancels. So “0 chosen arbitrarily” is exactly translation-invariance, and a never notices.

Theorem invariant under translations · checked, 105 trials

2But a single difference remembers the unit

Origin-free is not the same as ruler-free. Change the scale and the number moves.

why Under y → s·y:  s·Py − s·T = s(Py − T) = s·a. A signed length is homogeneous of degree one in the unit — invariant under translations, but not under the full affine group. It is a number-in-a-unit, not yet an invariant.

Theorem covariant of degree 1 in scale · checked, 105 trials

3Ratios forget the unit too

Take a ratio of two signed lengths along the same line and both free choices cancel at once.

why With c = B − Py, under y → s·y + k we get a → s·a and c → s·c, so a / c → (s·a) / (s·c) = a / c. This is the genuine affine invariant of the three collinear points (top edge, P, bottom edge): the first quantity in the figure that depends on the configuration alone, not on the ruler.

Theorem invariant under the full affine group · rel. error < 10−13, 5×105 trials

4Orientation is just s = −1

The downward–y convention is not a new phenomenon. Flipping the axis is the negative-scale instance of the two rules above.

why y → −y is the case s = −1 of rungs 2 and 3. So every signed length flips sign — a → −a, c → −c — and every ratio is untouched: a / c → a / c. The downward convention therefore does exactly one job: it makes “P below the top edge” read as a positive drop rather than a negative one.

Theorem sign flip + ratio fixed under y → −y
Observation “its only job is to make the drop positive” is an interpretive gloss, not a theorem

5One floor up: the cross-ratio

The ratio in rung 3 survives the affine group. Add the remaining projective freedom and it breaks — but something larger survives in its place.

why The full Möbius group y → (αy + β) / (γy + δ) contains the affine maps as the case γ = 0. Under a genuine Möbius map (with γ ≠ 0) the three-point ratio moves — a worked instance sends 0.5 → 1.5. What survives the whole group is the cross-ratio of four points. So the figure carries three rungs: difference (origin-free) → ratio of differences (affine: origin- and unit-free) → cross-ratio (projective). The six values the cross-ratio takes under reordering are the S3 orbit the rest of the suite is built on.

Theorem cross-ratio invariant under the projective group · rel. error < 10−12, 5×105 trials · three-point ratio shown not projective-invariant by counterexample

Net: the picture carries one cosmetic freedom and two substantive ones. The signed length spends the origin (and reads the cosmetic orientation as a sign). The ratio of two signed lengths spends the unit as well, and is the first thing here that belongs to the configuration rather than to the ruler. The cross-ratio spends what projective freedom is left. Nothing in the construction depends on the ruler we happened to paint — which is the whole reason these are the quantities worth building on.
