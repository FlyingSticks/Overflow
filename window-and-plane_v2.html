The Window and the Plane

Why the point has to be free to leave the rectangle

The split rectangle looks like the whole stage. It isn't. It's a window — a single bright room in a much larger plane, and the four edges that frame it are not boundaries at all but four infinite lines. The rectangle is just the one room where all four lines happen to face the same way. To see what the cross‑ratio is really doing, we have to open the window and let the point walk out.

1 Each length watches a line

Read the four lengths as signed measurements: a = Py − T is the drop from the top edge down to P, and likewise c from the bottom, b from the left, d from the right. Each one is pinned to a single edge — and that edge is really the whole horizontal or vertical line through it. A length is positive on one side of its line, zero on it, negative on the far side. a doesn't care about the rectangle; it watches one line and reports which side of it P is on. Inside the room, all four happen to read positive at once. That agreement is the only thing that makes the inside special.

2 Sign is geography

Now the whole picture is just bookkeeping on those four lines. Their crossing cuts the plane into nine rooms, and the sign of the cross‑ratio is fixed by one fact: Λ = ad/cb carries the sign of all four lengths multiplied together, so sign Λ = (−1) (number of negative lengths). At home, none are negative: Λ > 0. Step into one of the four edge bands and exactly one length flips: Λ < 0. Reach a far corner block and two flip, so Λ swings positive again. None of this is decreed by a formula laid over the figure — it is read straight off where P is standing. Sign is geography.

That settles the question the rectangle could never answer. The harmonic value Λ = −1 needs Λ negative, which needs an odd number of sign flips, which can only happen in an edge band — outside the rectangle. Locked inside, −1 is not merely hard to find; it is unreachable, and any figure that pinned P indoors could only assert the harmonic case, never show it. Open the window and it appears on its own, on the hyperbola that threads those purple bands.

3 The same exile, on a bare line

Strip away the second dimension and the same fact stands in its plainest dress. Fix two points A and B. The harmonic conjugate of a third point C is the fourth point D that makes the cross‑ratio (A,B;C,D) equal to −1. Slide C and watch where D must go: whenever C sits between A and B, its partner D is thrown outside; bring C to the midpoint and D escapes to infinity. The exile of −1 you just saw in two dimensions is this one‑dimensional fact wearing an extra coat.

4 The one shared point

It is tempting to call these the same machine — both involutions, both reciprocal in look, both fixed at ±1. They are not. Harmonic conjugation is a motion of the line: a projective map, so it preserves every cross‑ratio and leaves Λ untouched. The complement‑swap is a relabeling: it sends Λ to 1/Λ, inverting the very quantity the other preserves. Brought onto one value‑line they part cleanly — Λ → −Λ for the motion, Λ → 1/Λ for the relabeling, fixed at {0, ∞} and {±1} — and only the second belongs to the anharmonic group. The seductive “±1 on both sides” was only the cost of parking A and B at ∓1; move them to 0 and ∞ and it vanishes. What survives is narrower and truer: Λ = −1 is the fixed point of the swap — the self‑paired value — and equally the place where the involution fixing {A, B} exchanges {C, D}. The relabeling and the motion agree at exactly one value, and that value is the harmonic one. That single shared point is the whole connection; there is no identity past it.

Ledger. Theorem: with signed lengths, sign Λ = (−1) raised to the count of negative lengths, so Λ < 0 holds exactly in the four edge bands and nowhere inside the rectangle (checked across the plane to floating‑point noise). Theorem: for A, B at −1 and +1, the harmonic conjugate of C is D = 1/C; it satisfies (A,B;C,D) = −1 and OC·OD = 1, so harmonic conjugacy is inversion in the circle on diameter AB — whence C inside forces D outside, and the midpoint maps to infinity. Observation: the harmonic locus of the rectangle lives in the same exterior bands these two facts describe, which is why the point must be free to leave. Theorem: the harmonic value Λ = −1 is the fixed point of the pair‑swap Λ ↔ 1/Λ — equivalently, the condition that the projective involution fixing {A, B} interchanges {C, D}. Caution: an identity of conditions, not of maps. Harmonic conjugation preserves the cross‑ratio (it is projective); the pair‑swap inverts it; forced onto one value‑line they read as Λ → −Λ and Λ → 1/Λ, distinct involutions fixed at {0, ∞} and {±1}, only the second anharmonic. The matching ±1 is an artifact of placing A, B at ∓1; at 0 and ∞ it dissolves.
