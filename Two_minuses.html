Two Minuses

One you turn around — one turns the line over.

Companion to “What Survives the Ruler”

The word “minus” does two unrelated jobs on a line, and they live in the line’s two group structures. The additive minus — the sign in P − T, and the sign of k — is an element: a directed length pointing the other way. The multiplicative minus — the sign of the scale s — is an operator: a reflection of the line, not a point on it. One is a thing on the line; the other is a map of the line.

Additive — in (ℝ, +)

−a is the additive inverse: the same displacement reversed. It is a backward-pointing element you can pick up and turn around. This is the direction of a segment.

Multiplicative — in (ℝ*, ×) = {±1} × ℝ+

As a scale, −1 is pure handedness — a flip, the {±1}, with no center: the direction of the line itself, its orientation. As the map ×(−1) it becomes a mirror — a reflection through a definite point. An operator either way, never an element.

2One sign is structural · the other is borrowed

The two minuses also differ in standing. The sign of s is intrinsic: it is the line’s orientation, and no choice of origin can touch it — slide the zero as far as you like and nothing flips. The sign of k is relative: a directed length only counts as positive or negative once you have picked which way is positive. Switch that, and every length’s sign flips at once, while its magnitude does not move. The move here is a pure flip — it reverses handedness without naming any point, unlike the mirror in panel 1, which fixed 0.

3A flip is a mirror with its center forgotten

A flip names no point; a mirror is a reflection through a definite one, x → −x + 2p, with fixed point at p. So a mirror is a flip with a center chosen — and the gap between them is exactly the additive constant: the handedness supplies the −1, a translation places the center, and the fixed point lands at p = k/2. Drag the center below. The fixed point follows it; the handedness never budges from −1.

■Where the two touch

They meet at one point, and it is the s = −1 rung from the ruler note seen from the inside. Any mirror negates displacements — and it makes no difference where the center sits, because a directed length is blind to the center exactly as it was blind to the origin. Send both endpoints through a mirror and a = P − T becomes −a, whatever the center. So it is the flip — the handedness alone — reaching down to turn every directed length around; the point the mirror is pinned at never enters. That is the one place the multiplicative and additive minuses engage.

So the ruler note’s three-way split holds for a reason: origin (k) and unit (|s|) are substantive freedoms, and orientation (sign s) earned its own slot because it is the one minus that is an operator. The plus-or-minus of k never needed a fourth axis — it is just which way you shifted, and which way is borrowed from the orientation that sign s already fixes.

And the third panel is the rest of it. A flip is that orientation with its center forgotten; choose a center and you spend a translation to place the mirror, its fixed point landing at k/2. Flip is handedness; a mirror is handedness given a home. The two minuses build everything here: one turns a length around, the other turns the line over — and only when you tell it where.
