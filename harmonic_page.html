The Anharmonic Interface · the harmonic −1

One number, four views

The cross-ratio is the single quantity projection cannot disturb. Among the values it can take, one is privileged: −1, the harmonic. What follows is one argument in four passes — first what the cross-ratio measures, then the harmonic pair as a reflection, then why that reflection is exact, and last the −1 read straight off the formula. Every panel is live; drag the points.

01First — what it measures

The midpoint that outlives measure

Start with a parallelogram: its diagonals cross where the ruler calls the midpoint. Open one pair of sides, then the other, tilting the figure toward a perspective view. The crossing Q slides off the metric midline — the grey ½ walks away — yet the cross-ratio (A, C ; Q, S) never leaves −1. That is the whole motivation. The metric midpoint is an artifact of the ruler; the harmonic −1 is what “the middle” means once measurement is set aside.

02Then — the pair as a reflection

Q and S as reciprocal images

Strip away the perspective scaffolding and keep just the line. Put the base pair A and C on a circle centred at their midpoint M. The inside point Q and the outside point S that read as harmonic are reflections of each other in that circle: their distances multiply to R². The same −1 you just watched survive is, on the bare line, this reciprocal pairing — Q a third of a radius in, S three radii out.

03Why — the product is exact

MT² = MQ · MS

The reflection asserts MQ·MS = R² exactly; here is the reason. Straight up from Q meets the circle at T; the tangent there, perpendicular to the radius, swings down to fix S. The triangle M–T–S is right-angled at T, and TQ is the altitude to its hypotenuse, so the leg squared equals the product of the two pieces it cuts. Since that leg is the radius, the product is R². Drag Q and the two right triangles stay similar while the product holds.

04And last — in the formula itself

The −1, step by step

Finally, read the harmonic pair through the cross-ratio's own arithmetic: four signed differences, a numerator (A−Q)(C−S) over a denominator (A−S)(C−Q). Three of the four steps point right; one, A−Q, points left — and that single change of direction is the entire minus sign. Drag Q, and S follows as its conjugate: the four lengths shift constantly, never equal, while the two products stay matched in size and opposite in sign. That is −1, spelled out.

·One fact

Four passes, one fact. The invariant that outlived the ruler, the reflection in the circle, the geometric mean under the tangent, and the signed steps of the formula are not four results but one — four ways of saying that A, C, Q, S stand in harmonic position. The −1 is the seam where measure ends and projective structure begins.
