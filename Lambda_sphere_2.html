The λ-sphere

Where the cross-ratio lives — and where it leaves the real line.

Drag λ in the plane on the left. The sphere on the right is the Riemann sphere of cross-ratio values. Its equator is the image of the real line; the six special real values sit on it as a regular hexagon, and the two equianharmonic values e±iπ/3 are the poles. The moving dots are λ’s whole six-value orbit. Keep λ on the real axis and the orbit stays on the equator; lift it off and the orbit lifts off too. Drag the sphere to spin and tilt your view — tip it down to bring the far pole forward.

What you are looking at

Theorem. Reordering the four points sends the cross-ratio through its six-value orbit { λ, 1−λ, 1/λ, 1/(1−λ), λ/(λ−1), (λ−1)/λ } under the anharmonic group S₃. The orbit collapses only at three places: the degenerate {0, 1, ∞}, the harmonic {−1, ½, 2} (stabiliser of order 2), and the equianharmonic {eiπ/3, e−iπ/3} (stabiliser of order 3).
Theorem. In the stereographic normalisation that sends e±iπ/3 to the two poles, the real line becomes the equator, the six real special values become a regular hexagon on it (degenerate and harmonic alternating), and S₃ acts by rigid rotations: the 3-cycle 1/(1−λ) is a 120° turn about the polar axis, each transposition a 180° turn about an equatorial axis through an antipodal pair.
Observation. The cross-ratio of four real collinear points is real, so a real value is exactly one you can build from honest cuts — it lives on the equator. The harmonic value −1 is real (on the equator, drawable); the equianharmonic value is at the poles, the unique symmetric value that has left the real line. Its defining quadratic λ²−λ+1 = 0 has discriminant −3, against +4 for the harmonic λ²−1 = 0.
Checked  Hexagon spacing exact 60°; the equianharmonic pair at Z = ±1; every generator a rigid rotation (det +1, orthogonal to 1×10⁻¹⁵); orbit collapsing 6 → 3 → 2 at the special values.
