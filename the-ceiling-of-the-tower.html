Anharmonic Interface · the group acts on itself

The ceiling of the tower:
when a group folds back onto itself

The six anharmonic transforms are verbs on the projective line. Promote them to nouns and a single new verb appears — composition. Climb once more, to the symmetries of that, and the tower stops growing. This page lets you do the climbing by hand.

§0  operations on the line

Six verbs that move six points

Pick a transform. It sends each special value to another — the harmonic triple {−1, ½, 2} shuffles among itself, the degenerate triple {0, 1, ∞} among itself. Two orbits, three points each. At this floor, the six are actions.

Nothing here is new structure — just six maps. The move that follows is the only one that matters: stop watching what they do and start treating them as things to be combined.

§1  operations become elements

The verbs demoted to nouns — and one new verb, ∘

Set the six side by side as a finished cast. The only operation now is compose: do one, then the next. Tap a cell to read g ∘ h — the convention is h acts first. Every row and column is a reshuffle of the same six: the table is a Latin square, which is what "closed group" looks like.

An operation of operations, exactly as named: ∘ takes two verbs as input and returns a third, seeing them only as elements. Among the six sit three self-undoing ones — the involutions p=1−x, q=1/x, r=x/(x−1) — and two rotations σ, σ² with the identity e. They are the moving parts of the next floor.

§2  operations on the group itself

Relabel, act, relabel back

To climb again, act on the whole group. The structure-preserving operations are its automorphisms, and each one is a conjugation: f ↦ g ∘ f ∘ g⁻¹ — "relabel the line by g, do f, relabel back." Choose a relabeller g below and watch the cast re-pin. e never moves; the three involutions get permuted.

§2½  the count, squeezed

Six ≤ |Aut| ≤ six

Two walls close on the same number. From below: the six distinct conjugations you just found. From above: an automorphism must permute the three involutions, and since they generate the group, that permutation fixes everything else. Choose where p, q, r go and watch the rest be forced.

at least — distinct conjugations 6

send the involutions somewhere — pick a permutation

at most — choices for three generators 6

Every permutation of the involutions extends — to exactly one automorphism, realised by exactly one g. Count of valid ones: 6. The walls meet: |Aut(S₃)| = 6, and every automorphism is inner. A group whose conjugations are all of its automorphisms — and which has no centre to begin with — is called complete.

§∞  does the tower fold?

The ceiling — real, but not a law

Climb the automorphism tower: G → Aut(G) → Aut(Aut(G)) → ⋯. Because S₃ is complete, the map g ↦ (conjugate by g) is already an isomorphism onto its own Aut — so every higher floor is another S₃. It folds. Most groups do not: ℤ's tower collapses to a point and stops.

A copy is not a fold

Cayley's embedding S₃ ↪ S₆ also "reproduces a copy of S₃" — but inside a group of order 720. That is a copy sitting in something strictly bigger, not a tower stabilising. Same word, different phenomenon; only the automorphism tower actually returns.

Not generic

Folding-back is the privilege of completeness, and it is rare. A pleasant exception inside an exception: Sₙ is complete for every n except 2 and 6.

Kept — theorem, demonstrated

S₃ is complete; its automorphism tower is constant. Built here from the table: trivial centre forces ≥ 6, three generators force ≤ 6, the squeeze makes every automorphism a conjugation.

Struck — false rhyme

Every operation-tower folds back onto itself. ℤ → ℤ/2 → 1 never returns. The fold is special to complete groups, not a property of towers as such.
