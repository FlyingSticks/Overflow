The Sign, the Horizon, and the Anharmonic Six

A companion to our exchange — each claim set beside the diagram that carries it

The sections below place a statement next to its picture, so the sentence and the figure can check each other. Nothing here is new beyond what we discussed; it is the same argument, arranged for looking back and forth.

Throughout, the colour holds steady so a value can be tracked from one figure to the next: purple marks the harmonic orbit {−1, ½, 2}, slate the degenerate orbit {0, 1, ∞}, and red marks −1 — the sign, the half-turn, the one place where inside and outside both appear.  Symbols: λ is the cross-ratio; with r = a/b and s = c/d,  λ = r/s = ad/bc.

§1 · The ladder of vanishing points

“n-point perspective” is a count: how many of a figure’s edge-directions meet at a finite vanishing point — how many have been drawn in from infinity. A flat tile carries only two edge-directions, so it runs 0, 1, 2 — a third would need a direction off the plane, which this tile doesn’t have.

The diagonals always cross at a finite point Q. Q is the tile’s centre, not a vanishing point — the tempting fourth point that must not be counted as one. A genuine third vanishing point would take a solid, not this tile, and even then the picture itself stays planar.

§2 · The sign is the inside / outside

The sign of the cross-ratio is the whole inside/outside fact; nothing else in the number carries it. Drawn on the projective line as a circle, the question is only whether the two chords cross.

§3 · Difference zero, sum zero

Your two equations, read through r and s: +1 is r − s = 0, the ratios equal, the difference gone; −1 is r + s = 0, the ratios opposite, the sum gone. Sum-to-zero means r and s are mirror images through 0 — and that reflection through 0 is the inside↔outside swap in algebraic dress, since negating a ratio flips the side you measure toward.

§4 · The harmonic range, (A, C ; Q, T) = −1

Set the four points at −1, 1, 0, ∞: endpoints A and C at ±1, the midpoint Q at 0, its harmonic conjugate T at infinity. Then (−1, 1 ; 0, ∞) = −1 — the midpoint and the point at infinity are harmonic conjugates with respect to the endpoints.

§5 · The two registers

The difference/sum pair widens into the two moves that generate the whole orbit. The complement λ ↦ 1−λ is the additive move — Reach — a reflection about ½. The reciprocal λ ↦ 1/λ is the multiplicative move — Turn — the half-turn, and exactly the inside↔outside swap.

Reach fixes ½, the self-complementary centre, and ∞. Turn fixes 1, the identity, and −1, the half-turn. So ½ reads as midpoint-like because it is the centre of the additive reflection, not because anything was measured halfway.

§6 · The whole set, on one frame

The six values are the orbit of any single λ under the anharmonic group. At these special places the orbit collapses to two of size three: the harmonic orbit {−1, ½, 2}, genuine four-point sets, and the degenerate orbit {0, 1, ∞}, a coincidence where the fourth point has landed on one of the three base points.

Each of the three swaps acts as a reflection, fixing one value in each orbit and so pairing them — (−1, 1), (½, ∞), (2, 0), one harmonic and one degenerate apiece. Only the three-cycle fixes nothing. And across all six, −1 stands alone on the negative side: the single value that both carries the inside/outside split and is unchanged when inside and outside are swapped.
