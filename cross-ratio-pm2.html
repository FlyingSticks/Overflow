A split rectangle and its cross‑ratio Λ = ±1

Point P cuts the rectangle into four cells. Measured as signed displacements, a = top, c = bottom, b = left, d = right, so a length turns negative once P crosses an edge. The complements ad and cb carry the cross‑ratio Λ = (a/b) / (c/d) = ad/cb. Exchanging the two complements sends Λ → 1/Λ; the configurations it fixes are Λ = ±1 — +1 equal areas, on the diagonal, and −1 opposite areas (ad + cb = 0), on the harmonic hyperbola you can only reach by dragging P out of the rectangle.

Drag anywhere to move P — including outside the rectangle, where cells turn negative (shown hatched). Or hold P on either locus and slide along it.

Side ratios

a/b = 0 c/d = 0

On the diagonal a/b = c/d (Λ = +1). On the hyperbola a/b = −c/d (Λ = −1) — same ratio, opposite sign.

Both balances are theorems. The four signed areas are the rank‑one array [a  c] ⊗ [b  d], so ab·cd = ad·cb = abcd for all P (verified to floating‑point noise). The complements are equal, ad = cb (Λ = +1), exactly on the diagonal; they cancel, ad + cb = 0 (Λ = −1), exactly on the rectangular hyperbola ξη = HW/4 through the same two corners — a curve that lies wholly outside the open rectangle, which is why the harmonic conjugate is born only when P steps outside and a length changes sign. The values ±1 are the fixed points of the complement‑swap Λ ↔ 1/Λ — two of the six special cross‑ratios, the pair belonging to this one involution of the anharmonic group.
