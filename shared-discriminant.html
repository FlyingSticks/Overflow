The Shared Discriminant

One quadratic, counted against a line — read once as a conic, once as a Möbius map.

Drag the dial. A single number τ drives both pictures through the same discriminant Δ = τ²−4. On the left it is a conic and Δ counts its real points at infinity — its asymptotic directions. On the right it is a Möbius map and the same Δ counts its fixed points on the real line. Negative gives the bounded, rotating, two-points-off-the-line case: elliptic in both.

What the dial does. The quadratic z² − τz + 1 has discriminant Δ = τ²−4 — the same number that classifies a conic by B²−4AC and a Möbius map by its trace. Both pictures read that one number the same way: count the real roots, 0, 1, or 2, and see them as crossings of a line. The conic crosses the line at infinity; the map crosses the real line.

Left. The conic is the section y² = (τ²/4 − 1)x² + τx + 1 — a circle at τ = 0, opening into an ellipse, a parabola at τ = ±2, then a hyperbola. Its asymptotic directions are the real roots of the leading form: none for the ellipse (bounded, no real points at infinity), one doubled direction for the parabola (tangent at infinity), two for the hyperbola.

Right. The fixed points are the roots of z² − τz + 1, so their product is 1: a conjugate pair on the unit circle when Δ < 0 (off the real line — a rotation), colliding at z = ±1 when Δ = 0 (one doubled point — a shear), splitting to a reciprocal pair on the real line when Δ > 0 (a source and a sink). The light arcs are the invariant flow.

What it says. Elliptic, parabolic, hyperbolic are not three borrowed names — they are one classification, a quadratic's real roots counted against a line, appearing in two arenas because both descend from a 2×2 matrix's eigenvalues: complex, repeated, or real. Ledger: the discriminant correspondence is theorem-grade, verified here. The older reading of the fixed points as foci stays a rhyme — focus-like, not foci.
