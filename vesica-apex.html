why the compass suffices · one equivalence, read six ways

Off the line, but two swings away

λ equianharmonic ⇔ λ² − λ + 1 = 0 ⇔ |λ| = 1 and |λ − 1| = 1 ⇔ 1/λ = 1 − λ (reciprocal = complement) ⇔ λ + 1/λ = 1 ⇔ apex of the equilateral triangle on [0, 1]

The harmonic value −1 is a directed length: it sits on the real axis, and a ruler laid along the line reaches it. The equianharmonic eyes sit at height ±√3⁄2 — off the line entirely, where no signed measurement can go. This is the boundary What Survives the Ruler traced: differences and ratios and the cross-ratio survive projection, but they live on the line. To reach the eyes you must leave it — and the compass is exactly the instrument that does, by swinging equal radii from the two endpoints of the given unit.

The last equivalence is the one Two Minuses anticipated. The additive minus is the complement λ ↦ 1 − λ, a reflection about ½; the multiplicative minus is folded into the reciprocal λ ↦ 1/λ. Generically these two operations disagree. They are forced into agreement at exactly one pair of points — and that agreement, 1/λ = 1 − λ, rearranges to λ² − λ + 1 = 0. The equianharmonic value is where the two kinds of inversion become the same act.

What is proven, and what is only pointed at

Theorem

The two unit circles centred at 0 and 1 meet exactly at ½ ± i√3⁄2 = e±iπ/3, the roots of λ² − λ + 1. Equivalently: a point is equianharmonic ⇔ it is unit-distant from both 0 and 1 ⇔ its reciprocal equals its complement. Elementary and exact — the intersection of two circles, verified here to machine precision.

Shown — live

The probe reads |λ|, |λ − 1|, the reciprocal, the complement, and λ² − λ + 1 as you move it; the three conditions light together only at the eyes, where 1/λ and 1 − λ both settle on the opposite eye. The construct button replays the straightedge-and-compass sequence that produces them from the bare unit segment.

Note — the register it belongs to

This is a constructibility fact about a single algebraic value, not a claim about perspective or the Engine. It earns its place beside the sphere's tetrahedron only as the planar shadow of the same collapse: j = 0 seen with a compass instead of seen as a solid. Two faces of one number; the identification between the faces is the cross-ratio, already proven.
