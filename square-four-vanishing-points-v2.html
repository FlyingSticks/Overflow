Four points on the horizon, and the square they vanish into

Two vanishing points carry the sides of a square; two more carry its diagonals. Place the side pair wherever you like — the diagonal pair is not free. It is forced to sit as the harmonic conjugate: the cross-ratio of the four reads −1 for every turn and every distance.

Ledger

Theorem (bisector)
The two diagonal vanishing points are the harmonic conjugates of the two side vanishing points: (V₁ V₂ ; D₁ D₂) = −1, independent of how the square is turned or how far the eye stands. The diagonals bisect the right angle between the sides, so the internal and external bisectors cut V₁V₂ in the same ratio — and internal-plus-external division in one ratio is harmonic division.
Theorem (closure)
PQRS is a complete quadrangle, and its three diagonal points are V₁ = PQ∩RS, V₂ = QR∩SP, and O = PR∩QS. Two of them lie on the horizon, so the horizon is its diagonal-triangle side V₁V₂ — and the remaining opposite pair PR, QS must cut that side at the harmonic conjugate: (V₁ V₂ ; D₁ D₂) = −1, for every P and every size, by projection alone. It coincides with the bisector pair by uniqueness of the harmonic conjugate — so Δ is now a redundancy check on a proven identity, rounding to zero because the two constructions name one point.
Note
At α = 45° the view is symmetric: the square sits as a diamond, D₁ lands exactly on the centre of vision C, and D₂ runs off to infinity — the textbook harmonic pair { midpoint, point at infinity } of the two symmetric points. This is the harmonic value λ = −1, whose six-fold S₃ orbit collapses to {−1, 2, ½}.
